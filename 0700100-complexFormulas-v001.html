16  Tips for working with formulas

The following are approaches to help you work with formulas.

The “Formulas” tab contains several tools for working with formulas. Some of these are described below.

16.1 show-formulas button

It can be very helpful to see all the formulas in your spreadsheet at once.

To so, on the “Formulas” tab, press the “Show Formulas” button. Note that this displays what Excel actually stores as opposed to the values that Excel calculates. For this reason, when looking at the spreadsheet in the “formulas” view, dates will display as “serial numbers” instead of actual dates.

If you press the button again, the spreadsheet will revert back to showing the calculated values. We say that the button “toggles” between showing the formulas (AKA the “formulas view”) and showing the values (AKA the “values view”).

You can also press the following hotkey to toggle between formulas and values:

Windows: ctrl-`

Mac: Option+Command+`

Note that the ` is NOT the apostrophe, it is the back-tick or “accent” character. It is on the same key as the ~ character. On most american keyboards it is right under the ESC key.

16.2 Working with complex formulas

It is often helpful when trying to analyze a complex formula to see the arguments in the formula on multiple lines. You can make the formula bar on top of the Excel window larger by pointing at the bottom edge of the formula bar (the cursor will change to a double-headed-arrow) and then drag down. If you then press Alt-Enter (win) or Ctrl+Option+Enter (mac) you will be able to type the formula on multiple lines.

See the following for an example of writing a complex formula using this technique.

16.3 Lists of different functions

Click on the Formulas tab to see different categories of formulas in drop-down-lists. Click on a category and then click on a particular function to see a window that describes the different arguments for that function. If you click in a textbox for an argument you will see a description of what to enter for that argument. You can fill in each argument by typing or by clicking on the cells with your mouse. When you are done, press OK to see the results.

16.4 Trace Precedents, Trace Dependents, Remove Arrows buttons

Select a cell (i.e. click on it) that contains a formula. Then on the “Formulas” tab press the Trace Precedents button. You will then see arrows pointing to the cells that are referenced by the formula. If you continue to click this button you will see more arrows that point to 2nd level dependencies, etc.

Select ANY cell. Then click on the “Trace Dependents” button. You will see arrows that point to any cell that references the selected cell. If you keep pressing this button you will see additional arrows drawn to cells that reference those cells, etc.

None oft these arrows affect the way the spreadsheet works. They are simply there to help you understand the spreadsheet.

Click the “Remove Arrows” button to remove all the arrows from the screen.

16.5 Evaluate formula button

It can be helpful to see step by step how a formula is calculated. To so, select a cell that contains a formula (i.e. click on the cell). Then on the “Formulas” tab, press the “Evaluate Formula” button. You will see a window open up that shows the formula with part of the formula underlined. Click on the “Evaluate” button to evaluate JUST that part of the formula. Keep clicking on the Evaluate button to see step by step how the formula gets processed.
